## SECTION 1: TCS HIRING PROCESS & ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA

### 1.1 Academic Eligibility Matrix
To be eligible for the TCS National Qualifier Test (NQT) and subsequent interview stages, candidates must strictly satisfy the following criteria. Any discrepancy discovered during background verification results in immediate onboarding cancellation:

*   **Minimum Aggregate Marks:** A minimum of 60% or a 6.0 CGPA overall across Class 10th, Class 12th, Diploma (if applicable), Graduation, and Post-Graduation. The aggregate must include all subjects across all semesters; elective marks cannot be omitted.
*   **Active Backlogs / Arrears:** Maximum of 1 active backlog or arrear is permitted at the time of appearing for the TCS selection process. However, all pending backlogs must be cleared with passing certificates prior to the date of joining.
*   **Academic Gap Limits:** The overall academic gap from Class 10th to the highest qualification must not exceed 24 months (2 years). All academic gaps must be declared transparently with valid documentation (e.g., medical certificates, official gap affidavits).
*   **Course Mode:** Only full-time regular courses are accepted. Part-time, correspondence, or distance-learning programs are completely disqualified.
*   **Age Threshold:** Candidates must be between 18 and 28 years of age at the time of registration.
*   **Work Experience:** Freshers and experienced professionals with up to 2 years of relevant industry experience are eligible.

### 3.2 Sample Aptitude Questions with Mathematical Solutions

#### Question 1 (Time & Work - Foundation Level)
*   **Problem Statement:** Pipe A can fill an empty cistern in 6 hours, while Pipe B can empty the same fully filled cistern in 9 hours. Due to an operational oversight, both pipes are opened simultaneously when the cistern is empty. After 3 hours, an engineer notices the error and closes Pipe B. What is the total duration of time (in hours) required to fill the cistern completely from the absolute start?
*   **Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:**
    1. Let the total volumetric capacity of the cistern be the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of the given individual times ($6$ and $9$).
       $$\text{Total Capacity} = \text{LCM}(6, 9) = 18 \text{ units}$$
    2. Determine the standalone operational efficiency per hour for each pipe:
       $$\text{Efficiency of Pipe A} = \frac{18 \text{ units}}{6 \text{ hours}} = +3 \text{ units/hour}$$
       $$\text{Efficiency of Pipe B} = \frac{18 \text{ units}}{9 \text{ hours}} = -2 \text{ units/hour (negative indicates emptying mechanism)}$$
    3. Calculate the net filling speed when both pipes operate concurrently for the initial phase:
       $$\text{Net Efficiency (A + B)} = 3 + (-2) = +1 \text{ unit/hour}$$
    4. Compute the amount of work completed during the initial 3-hour period:
       $$\text{Volume Filled in 3 hours} = 1 \text{ unit/hour} \times 3 \text{ hours} = 3 \text{ units}$$
    5. Calculate the remaining unfilled volume of the cistern:
       $$\text{Remaining Volume} = 18 \text{ units} - 3 \text{ units} = 15 \text{ units}$$
    6. Since Pipe B is closed, Pipe A operates alone to fill the remaining volume. Compute the time needed for this step:
       $$\text{Time taken by A alone} = \frac{\text{Remaining Volume}}{\text{Efficiency of A}} = \frac{15 \text{ units}}{3 \text{ units/hour}} = 5 \text{ hours}$$
    7. Calculate the total elapsed time from the start:
       $$\text{Total Time} = \text{Initial Time with both open} + \text{Final Time with A alone} = 3 \text{ hours} + 5 \text{ hours} = 8 \text{ hours}$$
*   **Final Answer:** 8 hours.

#### Question 2 (Permutations & Combinations - Advanced Level)
*   **Problem Statement:** How many unique 6-digit even integers can be constructed using the digits $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 8\}$ such that no single digit is repeated within any individual number configuration?
*   **Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:**
    1. To satisfy the even number condition, the unit's place (the last position) must be filled by an even digit from the available set. The eligible digits are $\{0, 2, 8\}$.
    2. Because the digit '$0$' carries structural constraints (it cannot occupy the most significant digit position at the far left), we must split the problem into two mutually exclusive, exhaustive scenarios.
    3. **Scenario I: The unit's place is explicitly filled by the digit '$0$'.**
       *   Positions layout: `_ _ _ _ _ 0`
       *   The unit's position is fixed in $1$ way.
       *   The remaining 5 positions at the left must be filled using 5 distinct digits chosen from the surviving set $\{1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 8\}$ (which contains 6 digits).
       *   Number of valid arrangements for Scenario I:
           $$P(6, 5) = \frac{6!}{(6-5)!} = 6 \times 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 = 720 \text{ ways}$$
    4. **Scenario II: The unit's place is filled by either '$2$' or '$8$'.**
       *   Positions layout: `_ _ _ _ _ [2 or 8]`
       *   The unit's position can be filled in $2$ distinct ways.
       *   Now evaluate the leftmost position (the most significant digit). It cannot be '$0$', and it cannot be the digit placed in the unit's position. This leaves $7 - 2 = 5$ eligible choices.
       *   Next, fill the remaining middle 4 positions. The digit '$0$' is now allowed. Out of the original 7 digits, 2 digits have been consumed (one for the unit's place, one for the leftmost place). This leaves exactly $7 - 2 = 5$ digits available. We must arrange 4 positions from these 5 options:
           $$\text{Ways for middle positions} = P(5, 4) = \frac{5!}{(5-4)!} = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 = 120 \text{ ways}$$
       *   Calculate the total arrangements for Scenario II:
           $$\text{Total for Scenario II} = 5 \text{ (first position)} \times 120 \text{ (middle positions)} \times 2 \text{ (unit position)} = 1200 \text{ ways}$$
    5. Sum the mutually exclusive configurations to find the total:
       $$\text{Total Valid 6-Digit Even Numbers} = \text{Scenario I} + \text{Scenario II} = 720 + 1200 = 1920 \text{ ways}$$
*   **Final Answer:** 1920.

### 4.2 Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) Architectural Concepts
Interviews test the four core pillars of OOP alongside concrete design scenarios:
*   **Encapsulation:** Restricting direct access to an object's internal state to prevent unauthorized modifications. Implement this by declaring variables as `private` and exposing access via public getter and setter methods.
*   **Abstraction:** Hiding complex low-level implementation details and exposing only essential functional interfaces.
    *   *Abstract Class vs Interface:* Abstract classes can maintain state (instance variables) and contain both implemented methods and abstract methods. Interfaces represent pure behavioral contracts, supporting multiple inheritance. In modern Java, interfaces can also include default and static implementations.
*   **Inheritance:** The mechanism by which a subclass derives attributes and behaviors from a parent superclass, promoting code reuse. Types include Single, Multilevel, and Hierarchical. Multiple inheritance via classes is blocked in languages like Java to avoid the Diamond Problem (ambiguity during method resolution).
*   **Polymorphic Behavior:**
    *   *Compile-time Polimonphism (Method Overloading):* Multiple methods within the same class share an identical name but have different parameter lists (signatures differ by type, count, or sequence).
    *   *Runtime Polymorphism (Method Overriding):* A subclass provides a specific implementation for a method that is already defined in its parent superclass. The method resolution happens dynamically at runtime based on the actual object instance, not the reference variable type.

### 4.3 Database Management Systems (DBMS) & Relational Architecture
*   **The ACID Model:**
    *   **Atomicity:** Guarantees that an entire transaction executes successfully, or none of it does; there are no partial saves.
    *   **Consistency:** Ensures that a transaction transitions the database from one valid state to another, maintaining all schema constraints.
    *   **Isolation:** Concurrent transactions execute without cross-contamination or data leaks between states.
    *   **Durability:** Once a transaction is committed, its changes are permanently written to non-volatile storage, surviving subsequent system failures.
*   **Schema Normalization:** A systematic design technique used to organize data and minimize redundancy and update anomalies:
    *   **1NF:** Each column must hold atomic, indivisible values, and entries must be uniquely identifiable.
    *   **2NF:** Must satisfy 1NF, and all non-prime attributes must depend entirely on the whole primary key, eliminating partial dependencies.
    *   **3NF:** Must satisfy 2NF, and no non-prime attribute can have a transitive dependency on the primary key via another non-prime attribute.
*   **Indexing Fundamentals:** Using structural references (B-Trees or Hash Indexes) to accelerate query speeds by avoiding full table scans.

### 4.4 Operating System (OS) Fundamentals
*   **Process vs Thread Architecture:** A process is an independent execution unit with its own dedicated memory space allocated by the OS. A thread is a lightweight path of execution within a process that shares the parent process's memory space, heaps, and system resources. This shared space allows for fast communication but requires careful synchronization to prevent data race conditions.
*   **Deadlock Conditions:** A state where a set of processes are permanently blocked because each process holds a resource while waiting for another resource held by another process in the same set. A deadlock requires four conditions to occur simultaneously:
    1. **Mutual Exclusion:** Resources cannot be shared; only one process can hold a resource at a time.
    2. **Hold and Wait:** A process holding allocated resources can request additional resources without releasing its current ones.
    3. **No Preemption:** Resources cannot be forcibly taken from a process; they must be released voluntarily.
    4. **Circular Wait:** A closed loop of processes exists where each process waits for a resource held by the next process in the chain.

### 5.1 Program 1: Subarray Maximum Product Optimization
*   **Problem Context:** Given an unsorted integer array containing both positive and negative integers, isolate the contiguous subarray that yields the absolute maximum product and return that product value.
*   **Source Code Implementation (Python 3):**

```python
def find_maximum_subarray_product(nums: list[int]) -> int:
    """
    Computes the maximum product of a contiguous subarray using a 
    variation of Kadane's Algorithm to account for negative numbers.
    Time Complexity: O(N) where N is the length of the array.
    Space Complexity: O(1) auxiliary space.
    """
    if not nums:
        return 0
        
    global_max = nums[0]
    current_max = nums[0]
    current_min = nums[0]
    
    for i in range(1, len(nums)):
        current_val = nums[i]
        
        # If the current value is negative, multiplying by it swaps the 
        # potential maximum and minimum products.
        if current_val < 0:
            current_max, current_min = current_min, current_max
            
        # Evaluate whether to start a new subarray at the current element 
        # or extend the existing one.
        current_max = max(current_val, current_max * current_val)
        current_min = min(current_val, current_min * current_val)
        
        # Update the overall maximum found across the entire array iteration.
        global_max = max(global_max, current_max)
        
    return global_max

5.2 Program 2: Matrix Rotation without Memory AllocationProblem Context: Given a square $N \times N$ two-dimensional matrix, rotate the data structures by 90 degrees in a clockwise direction. The transformation must happen in-place; allocating a secondary matrix is prohibited.Source Code Implementation (Python 3):Pythondef rotate_matrix_clockwise_90(matrix: list[list[int]]) -> None:
    """
    Modifies the input matrix in-place by transposing it first, 
    and then reversing each individual row.
    Time Complexity: O(N^2) where N is the row/column count.
    Space Complexity: O(1) absolute auxiliary space.
    """
    n = len(matrix)
    
    # Step 1: Transpose the matrix (swap matrix[i][j] with matrix[j][i])
    for i in range(n):
        for j in range(i + 1, n):
            matrix[i][j], matrix[j][i] = matrix[j][i], matrix[i][j]
            
    # Step 2: Reverse each row individually to complete the clockwise rotation
    for i in range(n):
        matrix[i].reverse()

5.3 Program 3: Longest Substring Without Repeating CharactersProblem Context: Isolate the length of the longest contiguous substring within a given string that contains no duplicate characters.Source Code Implementation (Python 3):Pythondef length_of_longest_distinct_substring(s: str) -> int:
    """
    Uses a dynamic sliding window with a hash map to track character indices.
    Time Complexity: O(N) where N is the string length.
    Space Complexity: O(min(A, N)) where A is the size of the character alphabet.
    """
    char_map = {}
    max_length = 0
    window_start = 0
    
    for window_end, current_char in enumerate(s):
        # If the character is already in the map and within the current window, 
        # shrink the window by moving the start pointer past its previous index.
        if current_char in char_map and char_map[current_char] >= window_start:
            window_start = char_map[current_char] + 1
            
        # Update or record the character's latest position in the map
        char_map[current_char] = window_end
        
        # Calculate the current window size and update the maximum length found
        current_window_len = window_end - window_start + 1
        max_length = max(max_length, current_window_len)
        
    return max_length

5.4 Program 4: Balanced Parentheses ValidationProblem Context: Given a string containing only the characters (, ), {, }, [ and ], determine if the input string is syntactically valid based on correct ordering and matching closures.Source Code Implementation (Python 3):Pythondef is_parentheses_input_valid(s: str) -> bool:
    """
    Validates parenthesis grouping order using a stack data structure.
    Time Complexity: O(N) where N is the string length.
    Space Complexity: O(N) in the worst-case scenario where all are opening brackets.
    """
    stack = []
    mapping = {")": "(", "}": "{", "]": "["}
    
    for char in s:
        if char in mapping:
            # Pop the top element from the stack if it's not empty; 
            # otherwise, assign a placeholder character.
            top_element = stack.pop() if stack else '#'
            
            # The popped element must match the corresponding opening bracket type.
            if mapping[char] != top_element:
                return False
        else:
            # It's an opening bracket, push it onto the stack.
            stack.append(char)
            
    # A completely balanced string results in an empty stack.
    return len(stack) == 0
